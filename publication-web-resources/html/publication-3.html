2025 YEAR IN REVIEW

The Chesapeake School Board voted 7–2 to approve a controversial update to its professional conduct policy. The new regulation prohibits district em-ployees from providing students with preferred pronouns or titles that do not correspond to the student’s biological sex. Additionally, the policy pro-tects staff from “compelled speech,” stating they cannot be forced to use pronouns for colleagues or students that violate their constitutional rights or religious beliefs. While supporters view this as a protection of personal liberties and parental rights, critics and LGBTQ+ advocates argue it discriminates against transgender and non-binary individu-als, potentially fostering an unwelcoming environment.

In a major win for advocates, Virginia voters approved the constitutional amendment to strip the discriminatory language that defines marriage solely as between “one man and one woman” from the state constitution.

Though same-sex marriage has been protected federally since Obergefell v. Hodges (2015), the move to remove the ban from Virginia’s foundational document provides a crucial, permanent layer of state-level protection. The measure, which had been passed by the Democrat-controlled General Assembly in two successive sessions to qualify for the ballot, passed with a strong majority, indicating broad public support across urban and suburban swing districts. The measure still has to navigate through the Assembly before going to voters in November.

This year’s Human Rights Campaign Municipal Equality Index index was recently released, and for the first time three Hampton Roads cities scored a perfect score of 100. Virginia Beach, Hampton, and Newport News retained their 2024 perfect rankings. Norfolk scored 91, and Chesapeake scored 80.

Ahead of Trans Day of Remembrance on November 20, Advocates for Trans Equality (A4TE) released its 2025 Remembrance Report, honoring fifty-eight known trans people who have passed away this year. Twenty-seven were lost to violence and twenty-one to suicide. The report also honors eight trans people who passed from natural causes.

The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly

In late December the rainbow crosswalk in Virginia Beach’s ViBe Creative District were targeted in an act of vandalism. Located at the intersection of 19th Street and Cypress Avenue, the permanent thermoplastic displays were defaced with homophobic slurs, graphic imagery, and political messages, including “Trump 2028.” The community response was swift. Within hours, city public works crews power-washed the hateful graffiti, and local leaders, denounced the act. Advocates from Hampton Roads Pride also issued statements emphasizing resilience, noting that the crosswalk has stood for a decade as a symbol of dignity and inclusion.

T he US House of Representatives passed two landmark bills targeting gender-affirming care for minors. On December 17, the House approved H.R. 3492 (the Protect Children’s Innocence Act), sponsored by Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene, in a 216–211 vote. This legislation would make providing gender-affirming medical care to individuals under 18 a federal felony, punishable by up to 10 years in prison. The following day, the House passed H.R. 498 (the Do No Harm in Medicaid Act), which prohibits federal Medicaid funds from covering such treatments for minors. While civil rights groups and

medical associations condemned the moves as extreme, supporters argued they protect children from irreversible procedures. Both bills faced an uncertain future in the Senate.

In November the U.S. Supreme Court declined to hear an appeal from Kim Davis, the former Kentucky county clerk who gained national notoriety for refusing to issue marriage licenses to same-sex couples in 2015. Davis’s petition sought to overturn the landmark Obergefell v. Hodges decision, which established the constitutional right to same-sex marriage nationwide. The justices’ refusal to take up the case effectively upheld a lower-court ruling that found Davis personally liable for violating the constitutional rights of two couples. Davis was previously ordered to pay a total of $100,000 in damages, plus significant legal fees. The Court issued the order without comment, leaving the 2015 precedent intact.
