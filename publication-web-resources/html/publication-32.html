Coming of Age with a Bite or Two

BY DR. CHARLES FORD

Gerardo Samano Cordova’s first novel, Monstrilio, combines the tropes of magical realism, horror, and chosen family to provide one of the best stories that I have read in the past decade. Starting in Upstate New York, it travels to Mexico City, then to Berlin’s avant-garde art scene, and then to Brooklyn.

The genesis of this journey revolves around the sad death of a sweet eleven-year-old boy, Santiago, who was born with only one deformed lung. Magos, the emotionally obtuse artist mother, then decided to carve out part of that lung as a keepsake of the boy’s uniqueness, while her husband and Santiago’s father Joseph discovered what had happened and was overwhelmed.

That section of the lung eventually became Monstrilio, a hairy, hungry monster, who was nurtured and protected by an increasingly and explicitly queer set of chosen family members. Magos and Joseph as a nuclear, heterosexual couple was not enough to raise this scrappy piece of Santiago; by the end, we have Magos and Lena, and Joseph and Steve, as well as Joseph’s confirmed bachelor Uncle Luke.

Monstrilio or now simply M as a teen even had a boyfriend Thomas in Germany. Accordingly, except for the first explosive chapter about Santiago’s death, there is no one external narrator. Instead, there are four distinct parts driven by the four most significant characters: Magos, Lena, Joseph, and M. The four parts build upon each other with each one being better than the previous section in terms of showing the creation of M, a delicate and not-always obvious balancing act of enforcing assimilation to human norms and of celebrating difference, no matter how radical and dangerous that it

may be. In terms of enforced assimilation, the most graphic instance was Joseph’s approval of Lena’s surgical removal of M’s arm-tail, an unwieldly and unexpected appendage that accentuated the non-human aspects of their child. At the very end, nevertheless, it seemed like it was growing back.

The most unfortunate aspect of M, though, remained his insatiable taste for meat of all kinds, including tasty human flesh. Joseph tried to keep this proclivity under control, making M promise not to indulge. M tried, but Magos’

performance art rendition of their story in Berlin triggered M to eat their next-door neighbor Elias (and, most disturbingly to this queer family, Elias’s cat); Elias died before identifying M as his killer.

After fleeing Germany for Brooklyn with his family except for Magos, M worked quietly in a used bookstore, avoiding temptation until a co-worker got him a phone and invites him out. This opens up the Pandora’s box of gay male dating applications in which a succession of ensuing encounters with increasingly willing men induces M to bite and then eventually to eat his sexual partner. The novel ends with the juxtaposition of Joseph and Steve’s wedding with M’s being truly and horribly himself surrounded by queer community – the ultimate lavender paradox of convention co-existing with dangerous and unsettling impulses. While deftly exploring the themes of behaving and not behaving, Cordova’s art makes the seemingly gruesome palatable and the unfathomable grief of losing a child bearable.

Cordova, Gerardo Samano, Monstrilio (New York: Zondo Press, 2023), 336 pages. $17, paperback.
